The Champions League Final is the hardest ticket in club football. Harder than a World Cup final in terms of the ratio of demand to supply. A stadium that holds 67,000 people, split between two clubs whose combined fanbase can number in the millions, plus neutral tickets for UEFA partners, sponsors and hospitality.

The fans who get in are either very organised, very connected, or very willing to pay. This guide covers all three routes.

---

## How the ticket allocation actually works

UEFA splits the Final tickets roughly like this:

- **~17,000 tickets per club** — allocated to the two finalist clubs to distribute through their official channels
- **~16,000 tickets for official UEFA partners, sponsors and hospitality** — not available to general public
- **~10,000 neutral tickets** — sold through UEFA's official ticketing portal

The neutral tickets are the ones most fans are targeting, and the demand for them is enormous. In recent seasons, UEFA has received millions of applications for the neutral allocation.

**Club allocation:** If your team is in the Final, this is your best chance. Each club has its own system — usually prioritising season ticket holders, members with away game points, official supporters' clubs. The exact process is announced after the semifinal results. Sign up for your club's official communications if you haven't already.

---

## The timeline for getting a Final ticket

**Semi-final week:** The two finalists are confirmed. UEFA opens applications for neutral tickets almost immediately. This window is short — often 3-5 days. If you miss it, you're on the secondary market.

**Two to three weeks before the Final:** UEFA announces allocations from the neutral ticket ballot. Most people who applied don't get tickets — it's significantly oversubscribed.

**From the ballot to match day:** Secondary market prices escalate. Tickets that were €80-100 face value will be €400-800 on StubHub and Viagogo within days of the allocation being announced. By match week, expect €600-1,200+ for decent seats.

## What to realistically expect to pay

Let's be honest: if you're not a season ticket holder of one of the finalist clubs and you didn't win the UEFA ballot, you're paying secondary market prices.

For a standard Champions League Final, secondary market prices in the weeks before the match typically run:

| Category | Secondary market price |
|----------|----------------------|
| Behind the goal | €400-700 |
| Side sections (lower) | €600-1,000 |
| Side sections (upper) | €450-750 |
| Premium/lower tier sides | €900-1,500+ |

These are not prices people pay reluctantly — fans routinely spend this on the chance to see their team in the Final. But go in with open eyes about what it's going to cost.

## Is it worth it even if your team isn't in it?

This is a legitimate question, and the honest answer is: for most fans, not really.

The atmosphere at a Champions League Final is heavily influenced by which two clubs are playing. A Final between two clubs with huge travelling support (say, a Spanish club against an English club) creates an electric atmosphere. A Final between two clubs from smaller markets might feel flat by comparison.

If your club is in the Final, the answer is obviously yes. If you're going as a neutral, it depends on the matchup and how much you value the experience of being at the biggest game in European club football regardless of who's playing.

## Making the most of the city: Budapest 2026

The 2026 Final is at the Puskás Aréna in Budapest. This is worth knowing in advance because Budapest is an excellent city for a football trip:

**The city:** Budapest is one of the most impressive capitals in Central Europe. The Danube, the architecture, the thermal baths, the nightlife. For a Final weekend trip, you have one of the best possible host cities.

**The stadium:** Puskás Aréna holds 67,000 and is named after the legendary Hungarian player Ferenc Puskás. It opened in 2019 and is a modern ground with good facilities. Located in central Budapest, well-connected by public transport.

**Getting there:** Direct flights from most major European cities. Budapest is more affordable than most Western European capitals — hotel, food and drink costs are lower than Paris, Munich or London for a comparable trip.

**When to book accommodation:** The moment you know you're going. Hotels in Budapest for Final weekend will sell out. Book a cancellable rate as early as possible. If your team doesn't make the Final, you cancel; if they do, you have the hotel.

## If you can't get a ticket

There is a version of the Champions League Final experience that doesn't require a ticket: the fan zone.

UEFA sets up official fan zones near the venue with big screens, live entertainment and the match broadcast. Budapest is well-suited for this — there are large public spaces that can accommodate the fan zones.

The experience is genuinely good: you're in the city, you're surrounded by fans, you watch the match on a big screen, you're part of the atmosphere without paying €800 for a ticket. If your club is in the Final and you couldn't get tickets, the fan zone in the host city is significantly better than watching from home.

## One more thing: sort your travel before the draw

The correct order of operations for a Champions League Final trip is:

1. Your team reaches the Final
2. You immediately start looking at flights and hotels to the host city
3. You apply for UEFA neutral tickets within the application window
4. You look at your club's ticket process
5. If none of the above works out, you start looking at secondary market

What does not work: waiting until you have a ticket confirmed before looking at flights and hotels. By the time the ticket allocation is confirmed, the affordable accommodation is gone.

Book the hotel on a flexible rate immediately. If you don't end up going, you cancel. The cost of flexibility on a hotel booking is a few euros more per night. The cost of not having accommodation when you need it is far higher.
