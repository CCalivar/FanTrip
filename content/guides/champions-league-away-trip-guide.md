Champions League nights are different. There's something about a European away game — the unfamiliar stadium, the foreign city, the fact that you genuinely don't know what's going to happen — that regular league football doesn't quite replicate. If you haven't done one, you should. If you have, you know exactly what I mean.

But they require more planning than a domestic away game, and the margin for error is smaller. This is what I'd tell a friend who was doing their first one.

---

## Tickets: the earlier the better, and here's why

Champions League tickets move differently to league games. The away allocation for knockout rounds is usually between 2,000 and 5,000 seats — for a stadium that holds 60,000-80,000 people. That's a small number divided between fans who may be travelling from several countries.

**Where to actually get them:**

Official club channels first. If your club qualifies for a knockout round, they'll usually have a registration process for away tickets — supporters' club members, season ticket holders, away game points. Sign up for those communications now, not when the draw is made.

If you can't get official tickets, the secondary market is your only option. StubHub and Viagogo are the main platforms for European matches. Prices fluctuate a lot — I've seen the same match listed at €80 one week and €250 the next, just based on which team has recently played well. Buy when prices look sensible rather than waiting for them to drop, because they usually don't.

**One important thing:** always download the e-ticket to your phone and save a screenshot. Some stadium ticketing systems are finicky about live connections and you don't want to be loading a PDF in a car park.

## Which city are you going to? It matters a lot.

Champions League away trips range from extremely easy to logistically complex depending on where you're going. Here's the honest breakdown:

**Easy cities:** London, Barcelona, Madrid, Paris, Amsterdam. Direct flights from most European airports, good public transport, easy to navigate, lots of accommodation.

**Medium cities:** Munich, Dortmund, Milan, Rome, Lisbon. Direct flights available but may need to change. Some cities have transport quirks (Rome's stadium is not easy to get to, for example).

**More complex:** Some cities in Eastern Europe or away grounds that are a long way from the city centre. Worth doing extra research.

For any city, the single most useful thing you can do before you go is look up the official transport guidance from the stadium. Most major European clubs publish directions specifically for away fans, including which metro stations are safe to use and where away fans are expected to enter.

## Book flights and hotels as soon as the draw is made

The Champions League draw for knockout rounds typically happens several weeks before the matches. The moment the draw is made, prices on flights and hotels start moving.

A hotel that costs €90 the day before the draw announcement will cost €180 the day after if your team is involved. This is not speculation — it's a pattern that repeats every round, every year.

Book immediately after the draw. If you're worried about your team being knocked out before the match, most hotels offer free cancellation if you book the right rate. Pay a bit more for a cancellable rate rather than getting the cheapest option that locks you in.

Same logic applies to flights. Book refundable or changeable fares if you can justify the extra cost.

## What to expect on match day in a European city

**The away end:** usually in a corner or one end of the stadium, behind plexiglass or netting in some grounds, loud and tight in others. The atmosphere is almost always good — away fans who've travelled for a match are invested.

**Security:** stricter than domestic games. Bag checks, sometimes metal detectors, sometimes body searches. Don't bring anything you wouldn't take through airport security. Give yourself extra time.

**Food and drink:** varies wildly by country. German stadiums have excellent beer and food inside. Some Spanish grounds won't let you bring anything in and the food inside is mediocre. Italian and French stadiums are mixed. Check beforehand so you're not surprised.

**After the match:** this is when things can get complicated. In some cities, away fans are held inside for 20-30 minutes after the final whistle. This is for your safety, not an inconvenience. Don't try to leave early against instructions.

## The budget for a Champions League away trip

Rough costs for a mid-range trip from Madrid to London, Paris or Munich, including everything:

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Return flights | €80-150 |
| 2 nights hotel | €120-200 |
| Match ticket | €80-200 |
| Food and drink (2 days) | €80-120 |
| Transport (local) | €20-40 |
| **Total** | **€380-710** |

The ticket is usually the biggest variable. Group stage matches are cheaper (€50-100 on secondary market). Semifinals and finals are €200-500+.

To keep costs down: book early, use a zero-fee card like Revolut or Wise to avoid FX charges, and stay slightly outside the immediate city centre where hotels are 30-40% cheaper.

## Things that are worth spending on

**Travel insurance that covers match cancellation.** A Champions League match can be postponed. It's rare but it happens. If you've paid for flights and hotels and the match moves, standard travel insurance often won't cover the cost. Look specifically for a policy that covers event cancellation.

**A reliable eSIM.** You're going to need data for maps, for your e-ticket, for messaging your group. Roaming charges on some networks are still surprisingly high. An eSIM from Airalo costs €4-8 for the country you're visiting and gives you local data rates. It's the easiest €6 you'll spend on the whole trip.

**A hotel that's walkable to the stadium or easy to get to by metro.** The night of a big match, taxis near the stadium will be either unavailable or extremely expensive. Know your route before you leave.

## The thing nobody tells you

The best away trips in football are not about what happens in the match. They're about the preparation — having everything sorted before you leave, knowing your route, having your ticket ready, not spending the afternoon stressed about logistics — so that when you actually get to the ground, all you have to do is be there.

The match will be what it is. You can't control that. But you can control whether you enjoy the experience around it.
