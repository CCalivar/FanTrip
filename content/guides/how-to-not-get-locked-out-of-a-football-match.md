You've booked the flights. You've got the hotel. You've told everyone at work you're going. And then you're standing outside the stadium forty minutes before kick-off, watching the turnstiles close, because something went wrong with your ticket.

It happens more than you think. And it's not just inexperienced fans — people who've been going to away games for years still get caught out. The difference between getting in and not getting in is almost always preparation, and almost never luck.

This is the guide I wish someone had given me before my first major away trip.

---

## The ticket is the hardest part. Treat it like that.

The moment you decide you're going to a match — not "thinking about it," actually going — buy the ticket. Not tomorrow. Not after you check the hotel. Now.

For Champions League knockout games and derbies, the secondary market moves fast. Prices on platforms like StubHub and Viagogo spike as the match gets closer, especially in the last 48 hours. A ticket that costs €80 three weeks out will cost €180 the day before. And by the morning of the match, there are people outside the ground paying €300 for something they could've had for €70.

**What to do:**
- Check the official club website first — sometimes face-value tickets are still available even for big games
- If not, buy from StubHub, Viagogo or Ticketmaster as early as possible
- For Champions League away ends specifically, check if your club's official supporters' trust is allocating tickets — these are almost always cheaper and safer
- Always get an e-ticket that you can download to your phone *and* have a backup screenshot saved offline

The "backup screenshot saved offline" part is not optional. You don't want to be hunting for WiFi outside a stadium in Munich at 8:45pm.

---

## The people outside are not all sellers

One of the things nobody warns you about: the atmosphere outside big European grounds before a major match is intense. Not necessarily dangerous — but intense. There are touts, there are police, there are thousands of fans in various states of excitement, and if you're arriving to sort out your ticket situation in that environment, you will feel the pressure.

People buy tickets from touts outside grounds every week and nothing happens to them. But fake tickets are also sold outside grounds every week, and if you get one, you're not getting in. There's no recourse. The money is gone.

If you're buying from the street on the day, you need to verify the ticket on the platform it claims to be from before handing over any cash. Most platforms have a barcode checker. Use it.

The much better version of this story is: your ticket is sorted, it's on your phone, you walk past all of it without making eye contact, and you're in your seat watching warm-up.

---

## Get there early enough that none of this matters

Two hours before kick-off is not early. For a major European away game, two hours before kick-off is when the queues at the away end are already forming and the pubs nearby are already rammed.

The fans who have the best time at away games are not the ones who cut it fine. They're the ones who arrive in the city the day before, know which pub is the away fan meeting point, are in the ground 45 minutes before kick-off watching warm-up with a beer, and leave the stadium singing regardless of the result.

**Practical timing guide:**
- Arrive in the city: the evening before the match
- Get to the ground area: 2.5 hours before kick-off
- Be through the turnstiles: 45 minutes before kick-off minimum

If something goes wrong — and something always can — you have time to fix it. If your train is late, you have margin. If the queue at security is longer than expected, you have margin. The worst feeling in football travel is running.

---

## Document everything before you leave home

Before you leave for an away trip, this is what should be done, not in transit, not at the airport:

- Ticket downloaded and screenshot saved to camera roll
- Hotel confirmation email saved offline
- Address of the stadium saved in maps (not just searched — actually saved)
- Address of the hotel saved in maps
- Local emergency number noted somewhere
- Rough plan of how you're getting from hotel to stadium

None of this takes more than twenty minutes. But when you're in an unfamiliar city, your phone is at 12%, and you're trying to find the away end with kick-off in forty minutes, you will want all of this to already be done.

---

## The nervous energy is real — don't add to it

Big matches have an edge to them. The home fans are wound up. The away fans are wound up. Police presence is heavy. There are people looking for confrontation.

The fans who never have any trouble at away games are not the ones who are particularly brave or street smart. They're just the ones who aren't doing anything that draws attention. They're with their group, they know where they're going, they're not trying to sneak into the wrong end, and they're not engaging with anyone who looks like trouble.

That's it. Walk with purpose, know your destination, stay with your group, and the overwhelming majority of European football grounds are completely fine.

---

## One more thing: sort your money before match day

If you're travelling to a country with a different currency or even just to avoid the 3-5% foreign transaction fee that most bank cards charge — sort it before you go.

Cards like Revolut or Wise let you spend in euros (or any currency) at the real exchange rate with zero fees. On a €500 weekend this saves you €15-25. Not life-changing, but pointless to give away.

Get the card before you travel, add money to it, and you don't have to think about it again.

---

The principle is simple: every decision you can make before match day, make it before match day. Tickets sorted, travel sorted, money sorted, route sorted. Then the day of the match, the only thing left to do is enjoy it.

That's what these trips are supposed to be about.
