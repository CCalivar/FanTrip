The 2026 World Cup is in North America. 48 teams, 104 matches, 16 cities across the USA, Canada and Mexico. The final is at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey — the largest capacity ground in the tournament and the most expensive tickets in the history of the competition.

If you're thinking about going, you need to start thinking about it now. Not next year. Now.

Here's everything you actually need to know.

---

## The timeline matters more than anything else

World Cup tickets are sold in phases, and the price differential between phases is significant. Fans who buy in the first official sale phase pay face value. Fans who buy on the secondary market a week before the match pay three to five times that.

The rough timeline based on previous World Cups:

**2025:** FIFA begins processing ticket applications. There's usually a ballot system for high-demand matches — you register interest and FIFA confirms allocations. No guarantee of getting what you apply for.

**Early 2026:** Official sales open for matches where demand didn't exceed supply in the ballot. This is your last chance at face value.

**March-May 2026:** Secondary market prices start climbing as the tournament approaches. Group stage tickets that cost €80-120 face value are selling for €200-300.

**June 2026:** Tournament begins. Secondary market prices spike, especially for matches involving big nations. Final week ticket prices for anything beyond the group stage become extreme.

The lesson from 2018 and 2022 is clear: buy early in the official sale, or accept you'll pay a substantial premium on the secondary market.

## How much do World Cup tickets actually cost?

**Official FIFA prices (approximate, based on 2022 pricing adjusted for inflation):**

| Phase | Price range |
|-------|------------|
| Group stage | €60-200 depending on category |
| Round of 32 | €100-300 |
| Quarter Finals | €180-500 |
| Semi Finals | €300-800 |
| Third place | €150-400 |
| Final | €300-1,100 |

These are face value prices. Secondary market prices are typically 2-4x these amounts by the time the matches come around.

For a trip to see your country play three group stage games, budget at least €300-600 for tickets alone if buying officially. Double that if you're buying on the secondary market.

## Which city should you go to?

There are 16 host cities spread across three countries. For European fans, the choice involves more than just which matches you want to see — it involves transatlantic flights, and flying from one US city to another adds significant cost and time.

**For most European fans, the practical options are:**

**New York / New Jersey** — MetLife Stadium. 8 matches including the Final. Largest city in the tournament. Most direct flights from Europe. Expensive but lots of options.

**Miami** — Hard Rock Stadium. Great city, direct flights from Spain and most of Europe. Good for early-round matches.

**Los Angeles** — SoFi Stadium. Long flight from Europe but lots of direct routes. Semi final venue.

**Mexico City** — Estadio Azteca. Cheapest flights from Spain. Opening match here. The atmosphere will be extraordinary.

**Toronto** — BMO Field. Direct flights from London and several European cities. Relatively affordable compared to US cities.

The most cost-effective approach for European fans: fly into one city, watch 1-2 matches, and stay in that city for the whole trip. Internal US flights add cost and complexity.

## The secondary market for World Cup tickets

If you can't get official tickets or miss the sale windows, the secondary market is your only option. Platforms like StubHub and Viagogo list tickets for every match, with prices that fluctuate based on demand.

**What to know about buying on secondary market:**

Prices are quoted without fees. The final price including platform fees, delivery charges and payment processing is usually 20-30% higher than the listed price. Factor this in.

Delivery for World Cup tickets may be mobile entry only. PDF tickets may not be accepted. Make sure you understand the delivery method before you buy and that you'll have reliable mobile data at the stadium (buy an eSIM for North America before you travel).

Buy from established platforms rather than individual sellers. StubHub and Viagogo have buyer guarantee policies — if the ticket turns out to be invalid, they'll either provide a replacement or refund you. Individual sellers on social media or forums have no such guarantee.

## Why you need to think about the total cost of the trip now

A ticket to a World Cup match is not the whole cost of the trip. For a European fan going to the USA:

| Item | Estimated cost |
|------|---------------|
| Return flights (economy) | €500-900 |
| Hotel (7 nights) | €700-1,400 |
| Match tickets (3 group games) | €350-600 |
| Food and drink | €300-500 |
| Local transport | €100-200 |
| Travel insurance | €30-50 |
| eSIM / connectivity | €15-25 |
| **Total** | **€2,000-3,700** |

This is not a budget trip. But the fans who will have the most painful experience are the ones who underestimate the cost, scramble for tickets and accommodation at the last minute, and spend the tournament stressed about money.

The fans who will have the best experience are the ones who start planning now, buy tickets when they're available at reasonable prices, and arrive with everything sorted.

## The scams to avoid

World Cup ticket scams are a growth industry. With 104 matches and millions of fans from around the world, there are people specifically targeting football fans with fake tickets.

**What to avoid:**
- Sellers on social media, WhatsApp or Telegram offering "confirmed tickets" before official sales open
- Any listing that asks for bank transfer or cryptocurrency payment
- Prices that seem too good for the category they claim to be
- Sellers who can't provide proof of the ticket before payment

**Safer alternatives:**
- Official FIFA ticket portal (tickets.fifa.com)
- StubHub and Viagogo for secondary market (with buyer guarantees)
- Ticketmaster where available

If something feels wrong about a ticket or seller, it probably is. The money you save by buying somewhere slightly sketchy is not worth the risk of standing outside a World Cup match with a fake ticket in your hand.

## Set an alert and don't miss the window

The single biggest mistake World Cup fans make is assuming they have more time than they do. Official sale windows close. Secondary prices rise. Hotels sell out in host cities months in advance.

Register on FIFA's official ticketing portal now. Set price alerts for your target matches. And if you can commit to going, commit early — not when you can see the tournament on TV and wish you were there.
