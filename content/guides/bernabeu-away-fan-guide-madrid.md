The Bernabéu has just completed a €900 million renovation. The result is one of the most technologically advanced stadiums in the world — retractable pitch, 360-degree video screen under the roof, a glass facade that glows at night. Whether you love or hate the aesthetic, you can't deny it's an event.

For away fans attending a Champions League or La Liga match in Madrid, here's what you need to know.

---

## Tickets

Away allocations at the Bernabéu for Champions League matches are limited and distributed through the visiting club's official channels. For La Liga, visiting fans are allocated a section of the upper tier.

**Secondary market:** Both StubHub and Viagogo list Bernabéu tickets. Champions League knockout matches run €100-200 for the away section. For semifinals or historically significant fixtures, expect more. La Liga matches vary more by opposition — a routine mid-table match might be available for €60-80; El Clásico secondary market prices start at €300+.

**Important note:** Spanish ticketing has moved almost entirely to digital entry. Paper tickets are rare. Make sure your e-ticket works on your phone before you leave your hotel.

---

## Getting to the Bernabéu

The Bernabéu is on the Paseo de la Castellana, the main artery of Madrid, in a central and easily accessible location.

**Metro:** Line 10 to **Santiago Bernabéu** — the station is named after the stadium and is directly outside the main entrance. This is the correct way to get there from most parts of Madrid.

**From Atocha station** (main train station): Metro Line 1 to Nuevos Ministerios, change to Line 10, two stops to Bernabéu. About 15-20 minutes.

**From Barajas Airport (T4):** Metro Line 8 to Nuevos Ministerios, Line 10 to Bernabéu. About 40 minutes.

The stadium is also walkable from the centre of Madrid on a good day — it's about 25 minutes from Sol or the Retiro park.

## Before the match in Madrid

Madrid is one of the best cities in Europe for pre-match. The Bernabéu area itself has options but central Madrid is where you want to be.

**La Latina / Chueca area** — the best neighbourhood for tapas and bars before a match. A 15-minute metro ride from the stadium.

**Around Callao / Gran Vía** — central, busy, lots of options. Easy metro access.

**Salamanca district (near the stadium)** — the upscale neighbourhood immediately around the Bernabéu has restaurants and bars that fill with pre-match crowds. More expensive than central Madrid but convenient if you want to be close to the ground.

**What to eat:** Tapas in Madrid are among the best in Spain. A round of cerveza and tapas before a match — jamón, patatas bravas, croquetas — costs €2-4 per tapa in most non-tourist bars. Budget €20-30 for a proper pre-match feed with drinks.

## Inside the new Bernabéu

The renovation has transformed the experience inside the stadium. The most striking feature is the retractable roof, which closes for entertainment events and creates a different acoustic environment. For football matches it's usually open.

**Away section:** Visiting fans are allocated an area in the upper tier, behind one of the goals. The views from the upper tier of the new Bernabéu are excellent — steeper than before the renovation, which brings you closer to the pitch optically.

**The 360-degree screen:** The LED screen wraps around the underside of the roof and displays graphics, replays and statistics throughout the match. It's impressive as a spectacle, though some fans find it distracting.

**Facilities:** The new Bernabéu has significantly improved concourse facilities — better food options, more bars, faster service. Card payment accepted throughout.

**Atmosphere:** The Bernabéu has a reputation for being demanding and quick to whistle if Real Madrid underperform. For European nights against top opposition, it generates real noise — the Fondo Sur ultra section creates the most sustained atmosphere.

---

## After the match

Madrid has excellent night life and the city doesn't empty after a match — it comes to life. Bars and restaurants around central Madrid are open until 2-3am and beyond.

Post-match metro from Bernabéu station is crowded but runs frequently. Allow 20-30 minutes to clear the immediate stadium area before queuing for the metro.

---

## Where to stay

**Salamanca district** — upscale, closest to the stadium, walking distance in good weather.

**Malasaña / Chueca** — central, excellent neighbourhood, some of the best bars and restaurants in Madrid. 15 minutes to the stadium.

**Retiro area** — near the park, slightly more residential, good transport links.

Madrid is more affordable than London or Paris for accommodation. A good mid-range hotel in Malasaña or near the Retiro runs €80-130 per night.

---

## One thing to know

Madrid has two clubs. If you're in the city for an Atlético de Madrid match rather than Real Madrid, the Cívitas Metropolitano (Atleti's ground) is in the east of the city — same metro access principles but different line (Line 7 to Estadio Metropolitano). The atmospheres are very different: the Metropolitano is louder and more intense for most matches. Both are worth attending.
