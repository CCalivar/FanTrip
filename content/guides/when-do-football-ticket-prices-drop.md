Ticket prices on the secondary market are not random. They follow patterns that repeat across competitions and seasons, and understanding those patterns can save you significant money.

The short version: buy early or buy late, but never in the middle. The expensive time is the week before a match.

Here's what actually happens to prices.

---

## The typical price curve for a Champions League knockout match

Let's take a hypothetical second leg semifinal — a big match, sold out, high demand. Here's roughly what happens to secondary market prices over the weeks before:

**6-8 weeks before:** Prices are relatively high because there's limited supply available and sellers know demand will be there. But there are deals — sellers who listed early and haven't shifted tickets will sometimes reduce.

**4-6 weeks before:** This is often the sweet spot. Enough supply has come onto the market (people who couldn't go, corporate allocations being released, etc.) that prices are competitive. Not the cheapest they'll ever be, but reasonable.

**2-3 weeks before:** Prices start to climb as the match draws closer. People who were "thinking about it" start committing. Demand picks up.

**1 week before:** Meaningful spike. This is when prices go from "reasonable" to "expensive."

**3 days before:** Steep spike. The match is imminent, remaining tickets are few, and sellers know buyers are now committed.

**Match day:** Either very expensive (for premium matches) or sometimes bargains (for mid-table league games where sellers panic and drop prices).

---

## The exception: form-dependent matches

One thing that moves ticket prices more than timing is team form. If a team is on a good run of results heading into a knockout match, demand for their matches spikes.

A practical example: tickets to a club's home leg of a Champions League semifinal might be listed at €120 in the weeks after the quarterfinal draw. If that club goes on to win their next three league matches convincingly and the press is talking about them as potential champions, prices for that semifinal ticket will be €180 by the following week.

Conversely, a team that loses two consecutive matches and looks vulnerable will see prices drop, sometimes sharply, as fans who were planning to go reconsider.

---

## The different patterns by competition

**Champions League knockout stage:** Prices almost always rise as the match approaches. These are events with genuine scarcity. There is no match day bargain for a semifinal. Buy early.

**Premier League:** More variable. For big clubs (Arsenal, Man City, Liverpool) against big opponents (top-four rivals, their main rivals), prices are similar to Champions League — buy early. For routine matches or games between mid-table clubs, prices often flatten or even drop close to match day as sellers prefer to take a lower price than have an unsold ticket.

**Domestic cups (FA Cup, etc.):** More volatile. Early rounds are easy to get tickets for at reasonable prices. Finals are extreme.

**World Cup 2026:** Unlike club football, World Cup prices are expected to remain high throughout because you can't just go next month — the tournament is a one-time event with a deadline. Secondary market prices will be high from the moment official tickets sell out, and will only increase as matches approach.

---

## The one thing that always causes prices to drop

**Sudden bad weather or travel disruption.** If there's a train strike or heavy snow forecast on match day, a subset of ticket holders will sell even at a loss rather than deal with the disruption. For fans with flexible travel, these situations can occasionally create opportunities.

This is obviously unpredictable and not a strategy — but it explains some of the sudden price drops you occasionally see.

---

## Practical advice for different situations

**You know you're definitely going:** Buy as soon as you've confirmed your travel. The longer you wait, the more you'll pay.

**You're considering going but not confirmed:** Set a price alert. Don't check prices obsessively — it won't help. Decide your maximum budget and set an alert for when tickets hit that price.

**You haven't committed to travel yet:** Book the match first, then book around it. More than once, fans have booked cheap flights and a hotel "just in case" and then found tickets are out of their budget.

**You're flexible on which match:** You'll get better value attending a league match or a group stage game than a knockout round. The premium on knockout matches is real.

---

## The price spike you definitely don't want to catch

The single worst time to be still looking for a Champions League knockout ticket is the Friday before a Tuesday night match. Sellers know you're running out of time. They know alternatives are limited. Prices at this point are routinely 2-3x what they were four weeks before.

If you don't have a ticket by the weekend before, you need to decide whether you're paying the premium or you're watching on TV.

Both are valid choices. Just don't make the decision by accident.
