San Siro — officially the Stadio Giuseppe Meazza — is one of those grounds that you should visit at least once. Not because it's the most modern or most comfortable stadium in Europe (it isn't), but because it has history written into every concrete pillar and because when it's full for a Champions League night, there are few atmospheres in football that match it.

Here's what away fans need to know.

---

## Which team are you going to see?

San Siro is shared by two clubs — AC Milan and Inter Milan. Both play there, both use it for Champions League matches, and the approach for away fans is broadly similar for both. The differences:

**AC Milan:** Rossoneri colours are red and black. The Curva Sud (south end) is the main ultra section.

**Inter Milan:** Nerazzurri colours are blue and black. The Curva Nord (north end) is the main ultra section.

Away fans are housed in the Settore Ospiti — a section in the upper tier of one of the corners. The views from the upper tier are good; the facilities are basic.

---

## Getting tickets

**Official channels:** For both clubs, away allocations go to the visiting club's members and official supporters' clubs first. For Champions League knockout matches, these allocations are limited.

**Secondary market:** StubHub and Viagogo both list San Siro tickets. For Champions League matches, expect €80-150 for the away section. For big knockout matches (quarters, semis), €150-300.

**One thing to know:** Italian ticketing systems have been tightened significantly in recent years following safety incidents. Tickets are frequently personal (nominative) — your name on the ticket. Secondary market tickets for nominative games are risky; if the name doesn't match your ID, you may not get in. Check before you buy.

---

## Getting to San Siro

San Siro is in the west of Milan, about 6km from the city centre. There are two practical options:

**Metro:** Line 5 (purple line) to **San Siro Stadio** — this was specifically added to serve the stadium and is the cleanest option. About 25 minutes from central Milan (Centrale station area).

**Tram:** Line 16 from the city centre runs past the stadium. Slower but cheaper and serves slightly different parts of the city.

**From Milan Centrale (main railway station):** Metro Line 2 to Cadorna, then Line 5 to San Siro Stadio. About 30-35 minutes.

**From Malpensa Airport (MXP):** Malpensa Express train to Cadorna station, then Metro Line 5 to San Siro. About 60-70 minutes total.

**From Linate Airport (LIN):** Much closer — metro or bus, about 30 minutes.

## Before the match in Milan

Milan is an excellent city for a football weekend. Unlike some stadium areas, the San Siro neighbourhood itself doesn't have a great pre-match scene, so the smart approach is to spend the afternoon in central Milan and head to the stadium later.

**Pre-match eating:**
- Brera district: aperitivo culture, excellent spritz and finger food from 6pm, stylish neighbourhood
- Navigli: canal area, lively pre-match atmosphere, casual restaurants
- Near Cadorna station: practical location close to the metro change, several restaurants

**Aperitivo** is the Italian practice of free (or cheap) food with your drinks in the early evening. In Milan this is done properly — pay for a Spritz or Negroni (€6-10) and get a spread of finger food. It's one of the better pre-match meals in European football travel.

---

## Inside San Siro

**The experience:** San Siro is old. Built in 1926 and expanded multiple times, it's not a modern stadium in any sense. The concourses are dark and cramped, the facilities are basic, and the upper tier is steep. But the atmosphere, when the stadium is full for a big match, is genuinely special. The roar when a goal is scored echoes differently than in a modern ground.

**Away section:** Upper tier, corner position. Steep — some fans find the height and angle vertiginous. Views of the pitch are clear once you get used to the perspective.

**Food and drink:** Basic stadium concessions. Have your meal before you arrive. Expect limited card payment options — bring some cash.

**Security:** Italian stadium security is thorough. Bag checks, metal detectors, arrive early. For Champions League matches allow 60-90 minutes from when you enter the external stadium area.

---

## After the match

Metro back into central Milan is straightforward — Line 5 runs frequently after matches. The most comfortable option is to wait 15-20 minutes inside the metro station area before joining the main rush, or walk 10 minutes to the next station.

---

## Where to stay

**Central Milan** — Duomo, Brera, or Navigli areas are all excellent. Good transport links to the stadium.

**Near Centrale station** — practical for arrivals by train. Not the most characterful neighbourhood but convenient.

Milan is expensive by Italian standards but cheaper than Paris or London. A mid-range hotel in Brera or near the Duomo runs €100-180 per night.

---

## Something worth knowing about San Siro

There are plans to eventually demolish San Siro and build a new shared stadium for Milan and Inter. The timeline has been debated for years and it's still unclear when this will happen. But there's a real possibility that within this decade, San Siro as it currently exists will no longer host football.

If you want to experience the old ground before it changes, now is the time to go. The flaws are part of what makes it what it is.
